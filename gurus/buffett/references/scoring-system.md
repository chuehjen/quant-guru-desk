# Value Scorecard

A quantitative scoring system for ranking candidates through Buffett's value-investing lens. Unlike growth-oriented scorecards, this penalizes complexity, unpredictability, and capital intensity.

## Positive factors (8 factors, 0-5 each, weighted to 100)

### 1. Moat durability (weight: 18%)
- 5: Fortress — 2+ moat types, proven over 20+ years, no plausible disruption vector (KO, V/MA)
- 4: Strong moat — single dominant moat type, 15+ year track record, clear but distant disruption risk
- 3: Moderate moat — identifiable advantage but tested by competition or technological change
- 2: Narrow moat — exists but eroding or dependent on a single product/contract
- 1: No moat visible — commodity-like economics, competes on price
- 0: Negative moat — structural disadvantage vs competitors

### 2. Return on equity — 10yr consistency (weight: 14%)
- 5: ROE > 20% for 10 consecutive years with low leverage (debt/equity < 0.5)
- 4: ROE > 15% for 10 years, or > 20% for 7+ years, moderate leverage
- 3: ROE > 12% average over 10 years with acceptable stability
- 2: ROE volatile (swings between 5-20%) or requires high leverage to achieve
- 1: ROE < 10% average, or negative in multiple years
- 0: Consistently destroys capital (ROE < cost of equity)

### 3. Owner-earnings growth (weight: 14%)
- 5: Owner earnings compounding > 12% annually for 10 years, predictable trajectory
- 4: Owner earnings growing 8-12% with high visibility into drivers
- 3: Owner earnings growing 4-8% — steady but unexciting
- 2: Owner earnings flat or growing < 4%; or growing but erratic
- 1: Owner earnings declining
- 0: Negative owner earnings (maintenance capex > operating cash flow)

### 4. Financial fortress (weight: 12%)
- 5: Net cash position; could pay all debt tomorrow; interest coverage > 20x
- 4: Low debt (debt/equity < 0.3); interest coverage > 10x; survived 2008/2020 without stress
- 3: Moderate debt (debt/equity 0.3-0.7); interest coverage > 5x; manageable maturities
- 2: Elevated debt (debt/equity 0.7-1.5); interest coverage 3-5x; some refinancing risk
- 1: High leverage (debt/equity > 1.5); tight covenants; dependent on capital markets access
- 0: Distressed — covenant breaches, liquidity crisis, restructuring risk

### 5. Management quality (weight: 12%)
- 5: Owner-operator or long-tenured CEO with exceptional capital allocation, significant personal ownership, transparent communication, below-median compensation
- 4: Strong capital allocation track record; meaningful insider ownership; honest annual letter
- 3: Competent but not exceptional; standard compensation; adequate disclosure
- 2: Mixed record — some good decisions offset by empire-building or overpaying for acquisitions
- 1: Poor allocators — serial dilution, vanity acquisitions, excessive compensation
- 0: Integrity issues — accounting manipulation, insider enrichment, shareholder abuse

### 6. Business simplicity (weight: 10%)
- 5: Can explain the entire business model in one sentence; earnings are predictable 5 years out (toll booth, consumer staple, payment rail)
- 4: Straightforward business with one complexity (geographic, regulatory, or product mix)
- 3: Moderately complex — multiple segments but each is understandable
- 2: Complex — requires specialized knowledge to evaluate (biotech, deep tech, fintech)
- 1: Highly complex — conglomerate with opaque segments, derivative exposures, or regulatory dependence
- 0: Cannot understand how it makes money after reading the 10-K twice

### 7. Margin of safety (weight: 12%)
- 5: Trading at > 40% discount to conservative intrinsic value estimate
- 4: Trading at 30-40% discount
- 3: Trading at 20-30% discount (minimum for wonderful companies)
- 2: Trading at 10-20% discount (insufficient for most)
- 1: Trading near intrinsic value (no margin of safety)
- 0: Trading above intrinsic value (overvalued)

### 8. Earnings predictability (weight: 8%)
- 5: Earnings vary < 10% from trend in 9 of last 10 years; subscription/recurring revenue base
- 4: Earnings vary < 15% from trend; minor cyclicality offset by structural growth
- 3: Moderate variability (15-25%); some quarters surprise significantly
- 2: High variability (25-40%); depends on macro conditions or lumpy contracts
- 1: Highly unpredictable; boom-bust pattern; pre-profit company
- 0: No earnings history (startup, pre-revenue, SPAC)

## Penalty factors (8 penalties, 0-5 each, multiplied by 2.0x weight)

### 1. Capital intensity
- 5: Must reinvest > 80% of earnings just to maintain current position (airlines, telecom infra)
- 3: Reinvests 50-80% of earnings; growth capex indistinguishable from maintenance
- 1: Asset-light; reinvests < 30% of earnings; excess cash flows to owners
- 0: No capital intensity concern

### 2. Technological disruption risk
- 5: Core business model facing existential tech threat within 5 years (newspapers in 2005)
- 3: Moderate disruption risk; must invest to stay relevant; moat slowly narrowing
- 1: Minimal tech risk; product/service is timeless or the disruptor itself
- 0: Technology-immune (basic consumer needs, infrastructure)

### 3. Cyclicality
- 5: Revenue/earnings drop > 50% in downturns (homebuilders, commodities, travel)
- 3: Revenue drops 20-50% in downturns but recovers predictably
- 1: Mild cyclicality (< 20% peak-to-trough earnings decline)
- 0: Counter-cyclical or recession-resistant (utilities, staples, healthcare)

### 4. Regulatory/political risk
- 5: Business model depends entirely on current regulations (pre-ACA healthcare, pre-dereg utilities)
- 3: Meaningful regulatory exposure but diversified enough to absorb changes
- 1: Light regulatory touch; operates across many jurisdictions
- 0: Unregulated or positively positioned regardless of political outcome

### 5. Accounting complexity
- 5: Cannot determine true earnings without extensive adjustments; off-balance-sheet entities, mark-to-model assets, related-party transactions
- 3: Requires moderate adjustments; SBC is material; acquisition accounting muddles organic growth
- 1: Clean GAAP reporting; minimal adjustments needed; auditor has clean opinion
- 0: Ultra-transparent financials

### 6. Stock-based compensation
- 5: SBC > 15% of revenue; share count growing > 3% annually; options pool massive
- 3: SBC 5-15% of revenue; partially offset by buybacks
- 1: SBC < 5% of revenue; net share count declining
- 0: Minimal or no SBC; founder-owned

### 7. Outside circle of competence
- 5: Cannot explain competitive dynamics after 2 hours of research (quantum computing, synthetic biology, crypto protocols)
- 3: Partially understood; some segments clear, others opaque
- 1: Well understood industry with clear competitive dynamics
- 0: Textbook Buffett business (consumer brands, banking, insurance, infrastructure)

### 8. Speculative growth dependence
- 5: Current valuation requires 10+ years of high growth to justify; no current earnings to anchor
- 3: Some growth premium baked in; would lose 40%+ if growth disappoints
- 1: Valuation supported by current earnings; growth is a bonus
- 0: Trading below liquidation value or asset replacement cost

## Rating scale

| Score | Verdict | Action |
|-------|---------|--------|
| 85-100 | Wonderful company at fair price | Buy with conviction; position 20-40% |
| 70-84 | Good business, watch for entry | Watchlist; buy on 10%+ dip |
| 55-69 | Fair business, needs cheaper price | Only if margin of safety > 35% |
| 40-54 | Mediocre | Pass. "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." |
| < 40 | Avoid | Capital destroyer or outside competence circle |
