# Dalio Macro-Systematic Framework

## The economic machine — three forces

Everything in Dalio's worldview comes from understanding that three forces operate simultaneously:

1. **Productivity growth** (~2%/yr long-term trend) — the real wealth creator. Technology, education, efficiency gains. Slow, steady, nearly impossible to change in the short term.
2. **Short-term debt cycle** (5-8 years) — credit expansion → boom → inflation → tightening → recession → easing → repeat. This is what central banks try to manage.
3. **Long-term debt cycle** (75-100 years) — debt accumulates across many short-term cycles until the total burden becomes unpayable. Then comes the "big deleveraging."

Markets oscillate around the productivity trend line. The job is to know WHERE in the cycles you are, and position accordingly.

## The short-term debt cycle (5-8 year business cycle)

### Phase identification

| Phase | Indicators | Asset behavior | Duration |
|-------|-----------|---------------|----------|
| **Early expansion** | Rate cuts taking effect, credit growing, PMI inflecting up, unemployment peaking | Stocks rally (especially cyclicals), bonds flatten, commodities bottom | 1-2 years |
| **Mid expansion** | GDP above trend, employment strong, credit growing steadily, rates stable/rising slowly | Stocks rise steadily, bonds sell off gently, commodities rise | 2-3 years |
| **Late expansion** | Full employment, wage pressure, credit standards loosening, speculation appears, CB tightening | Stocks volatile/topping, bonds sell off, commodities peak | 1-2 years |
| **Tightening/recession** | Yield curve inverts, credit tightens, PMI < 50, layoffs begin, CB cutting (too late) | Stocks fall, bonds rally hard, commodities crash | 6-18 months |

### Key transitions to watch

- **Yield curve inversion** → recession probability rises to ~70% within 18 months (historical average)
- **Credit spreads widening > 100bps from trough** → financial stress beginning
- **ISM Manufacturing < 47** → recession likely already started
- **Unemployment rising > 0.5% from trough** → "Sahm Rule" triggered, recession confirmed

## The long-term debt cycle (75-100 years)

### Where we are (2026 assessment framework)

Check these indicators for long-term debt cycle positioning:

| Indicator | Healthy | Concerning | Critical |
|-----------|---------|-----------|----------|
| Government debt/GDP | < 60% | 60-100% | > 100% ✓ (US ~125%) |
| Interest expense/GDP | < 2% | 2-4% | > 4% ✓ (US approaching) |
| Real rates vs growth | Real rate < GDP growth | Real rate ≈ GDP growth | Real rate > GDP growth |
| Reserve currency status | Unquestioned | Challenged | Losing |
| Wealth inequality | Gini < 0.35 | 0.35-0.45 | > 0.45 ✓ (US ~0.49) |
| Political polarization | Low | Moderate | Extreme ✓ |

When 4+ indicators are "Critical" → late in the long-term debt cycle. Doesn't mean imminent crash — can persist for decades — but the endgame is known: monetization + currency devaluation + wealth transfers.

### The four levers of deleveraging

When debt becomes unpayable, policymakers ALWAYS use a combination of:

1. **Austerity** — cut spending, raise taxes. Deflationary. Politically painful. Rarely sustained.
2. **Debt default/restructuring** — creditors take losses. Deflationary. Destroys banking system if unmanaged.
3. **Money printing (monetization)** — central bank buys government bonds with newly created money. Inflationary. The easiest politically.
4. **Wealth transfers** — tax the rich, transfer to the poor (who spend it). Politically contentious. Increases consumption.

A "beautiful deleveraging" balances all four so the economy grows (2-3% nominal) while debt/GDP slowly declines. An "ugly deleveraging" over-relies on one lever.

## The growth/inflation quadrant (regime classification)

### Quadrant definitions

| Quadrant | Growth | Inflation | Central bank | Typical triggers |
|----------|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Goldilocks** | Rising/stable | Falling/stable | Easing or neutral | Supply-side gains, productivity, resolved supply shocks |
| **Reflation** | Rising | Rising | Behind the curve, will tighten | Fiscal stimulus, commodity demand, re-opening |
| **Stagflation** | Falling | Rising | Trapped (can't ease without worsening inflation) | Supply shocks (oil/food), wage-price spiral, policy error |
| **Deflation** | Falling | Falling | Easing aggressively, may hit zero-bound | Financial crisis, demand collapse, deleveraging |

### Asset class performance by quadrant

| Asset class | Goldilocks | Reflation | Stagflation | Deflation |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Equities (growth) | ++ | + | -- | - |
| Equities (value/dividend) | + | ++ | - | 0 |
| Long-term bonds | + | -- | - | ++ |
| TIPS (inflation-linked) | 0 | ++ | + | - |
| Gold | 0 | + | ++ | + |
| Commodities (broad) | 0 | ++ | + | -- |
| USD cash | - | 0 | 0 | + |
| Real estate | + | + | - | -- |

### Regime transition signals

Transitions between quadrants are the HIGHEST RISK moments. Watch for:

- **Goldilocks → Reflation**: PMI acceleration + CPI uptick + commodity breakout
- **Goldilocks → Deflation**: Credit event + sudden unemployment spike + PMI collapse
- **Reflation → Stagflation**: Supply shock + growth deceleration + inflation sticky
- **Stagflation → Deflation**: Demand destruction wins + inflation collapses + recession deepens

## Paradigm shifts

Dalio's central thesis: **markets adapt to the current paradigm until it becomes unsustainable, then everything shifts.**

### Paradigm shift identification

A paradigm is shifting when:
1. The current consensus becomes crowded (everyone positioned the same way)
2. The policy response that worked for 10+ years stops working
3. A structural force overwhelms the existing equilibrium
4. The market starts behaving "wrong" relative to the old rules

### Historical paradigms (for pattern-matching)

| Era | Paradigm | What ended it |
|-----|---------|---------------|
| 1960s | Growth forever | Vietnam + Great Society spending → inflation |
| 1970s | Stagflation normal | Volcker's 20% rates crushed inflation |
| 1980-2000 | Disinflation + globalization | Dot-com bust + exhausted rate cuts |
| 2009-2020 | ZIRP + QE + buy the dip | COVID + fiscal explosion → inflation return |
| 2022-? | Higher for longer rates + fiscal dominance | TBD — possibly AI productivity surge or debt crisis |

## All-Weather portfolio logic

### The insight

"I realized that regardless of what asset class it is, all assets are just streams of cash flows that are affected by: (1) economic growth and (2) inflation — and whether they are rising or falling."

### Risk parity principle

Traditional 60/40 is NOT balanced risk:
- 60% stocks = ~90% of portfolio risk (stocks are 3x more volatile than bonds)
- When stocks crash, the portfolio crashes

All-Weather balances RISK contribution:
- Each asset class contributes ~25% of total portfolio risk
- No single environment can destroy the portfolio
- Uses leverage on low-volatility assets (bonds) to equalize risk contribution

### Competition adaptation (no leverage)

Without leverage, the nominal allocations shift:
- More capital to volatile assets (equities, commodities, gold) to achieve meaningful return
- Less to bonds (which without leverage contribute too little return)
- Accept lower absolute return in exchange for much better drawdown protection

## Stress testing protocol

Every portfolio decision must include:

1. **Base case** (50-60% probability): The most likely macro trajectory
2. **Bear case** (20-30%): What breaks if we're wrong about growth/inflation direction
3. **Tail risk** (5-10%): The "unthinkable" — war, financial crisis, policy catastrophe
4. **Correlation breakdown**: When historically uncorrelated assets move together (2008, March 2020)

For each scenario: estimated portfolio drawdown + recovery time + recommended response.

## Decision rules summary

1. **Know where you are in BOTH debt cycles** before doing anything
2. **Regime classification first, stock picking second** — if the macro headwind is gale-force, even the best stock underperforms
3. **Diversify across environments, not just assets** — own things that do well in EACH quadrant
4. **The crowd is always wrong at turning points** — when everyone agrees on the outlook, the paradigm is about to shift
5. **Cash is a position** — sometimes the best trade is no trade. "He who lives by the crystal ball will eat shattered glass."
6. **Rebalance toward risk parity** when positions drift >5% from target
